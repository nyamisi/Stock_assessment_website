Daily Egg Production Method (DEPM) - Sample Questions

AQ 232 - Fish Population Dynamics and Stock Assessment

Author

Nyamisi Peter

Published

February 11, 2026

Daily Egg Production Method (DEPM) Formula

\[SSB = \frac{P_0 \times A}{R \times S \times F}\]

Where: - SSB = Spawning stock biomass - \(P_0\) = Daily egg production per unit area - \(A\) = Spawning area - \(R\) = Sex ratio (proportion female) - \(S\) = Spawning fraction (proportion spawning per day) - \(F\) = Batch fecundity (eggs per spawning female)

Question 1: Basic Calculation

A sardine spawning survey in Tanzanian coastal waters collected the following data:

  • Daily egg production per unit area (\(P_0\)) = 450 eggs/m²/day
  • Spawning area (\(A\)) = 5,000 km²
  • Sex ratio (\(R\)) = 0.52 (52% female)
  • Spawning fraction (\(S\)) = 0.35 (35% spawn per day)
  • Batch fecundity (\(F\)) = 12,000 eggs/female

Calculate the spawning stock biomass (SSB).

Answer:

First, convert spawning area to m²: \[A = 5,000 \text{ km}^2 = 5,000 \times 10^6 \text{ m}^2 = 5 \times 10^9 \text{ m}^2\]

Apply DEPM formula: \[SSB = \frac{450 \times 5 \times 10^9}{0.52 \times 0.35 \times 12,000}\]

\[SSB = \frac{2.25 \times 10^{12}}{2,184} = 1.03 \times 10^9 \text{ eggs}\]

Converting to biomass (sardine eggs ~0.75 mg = 0.00075 g per egg): \[1.03 \times 10^9 \text{ eggs} \times 0.00075 \text{ g/egg} = 7.73 \times 10^8 \text{ g}\]

Convert to tonnes (1 tonne = \(10^6\) g): \[\frac{7.73 \times 10^8 \text{ g}}{10^6 \text{ g/tonne}} = 773 \text{ tonnes}\]

\[SSB \approx 773 \text{ tonnes}\]

Question 2: Parameter Interpretation

Explain what each parameter in the DEPM formula represents and why it is important for SSB estimation.

Answer:

  • \(P_0\) (Daily Egg Production): Measures spawning intensity in the survey area. Higher \(P_0\) indicates active spawning; reflects reproductive output per unit spawning habitat.

  • \(A\) (Spawning Area): Geographic extent of the spawning ground. Larger area = greater total egg production. Accurate delineation is critical.

  • \(R\) (Sex Ratio): Only females produce eggs. Must adjust for proportion of females in spawning population. Typically 0.48-0.52 depending on species.

  • \(S\) (Spawning Fraction): Not all females spawn on a given day. Varies by species and environmental conditions. Lower \(S\) = smaller daily fraction of stock spawning.

  • \(F\) (Batch Fecundity): Individual female reproductive capacity. Higher \(F\) = fewer females needed to produce observed eggs. Critical parameter often determined from histological analysis.

Importance: Each parameter affects SSB inversely or directly. Small errors in any parameter compound to large SSB estimation errors.

Question 3: Sensitivity Analysis

If spawning fraction (\(S\)) decreases from 0.40 to 0.25 due to poor environmental conditions, how does this affect SSB estimates? What does this imply biologically?

Answer:

Effect on SSB:

With \(S = 0.40\): Denominator = \(0.52 \times 0.40 \times 12,000 = 2,496\)

With \(S = 0.25\): Denominator = \(0.52 \times 0.25 \times 12,000 = 1,560\)

\[\frac{\text{SSB}_{new}}{\text{SSB}_{old}} = \frac{2,496}{1,560} = 1.60\]

SSB increases by 60% for same number of observed eggs.

Biological Implication:

If fewer females spawn per day (\(S\) decreases), a larger total stock is required to produce the observed daily egg production. This indicates:

  • Reduced spawning activity per individual female
  • Possible physiological stress (food scarcity, temperature extremes, pollution)
  • Recruitment failure or poor condition of females
  • Need for management intervention (reduced fishing pressure)

Question 4: Data Collection Problem

A researcher estimates spawning stock biomass using DEPM but realizes the sex ratio in the spawning population differs from the population overall. How would this bias SSB estimates?

Answer:

Scenario 1: Actual \(R\) > Assumed \(R\)

If more females spawn than assumed: - Denominator is underestimated - SSB estimates are inflated (overestimate of true stock) - Result: More liberal catch recommendations (dangerous)

Scenario 2: Actual \(R\) < Assumed \(R\)

If fewer females spawn than assumed: - Denominator is overestimated - SSB estimates are deflated (underestimate of true stock) - Result: More conservative catch recommendations (safer)

Why This Matters:

Sex ratio bias directly affects SSB by factor of (assumed \(R\) / actual \(R\)). A 10% error in sex ratio causes ~10% error in SSB.

Solution: - Sample spawning ground directly - Use separate sex ratio estimates from spawning area - Conduct sensitivity analysis on \(R\) uncertainty

Question 5: Application Question

A small pelagic fishery in Zanzibar wants to set catch limits based on SSB estimates from DEPM. What assumptions must be met for this method to be reliable?

Answer:

Biological Assumptions:

  1. Pelagic eggs: Eggs develop in water column (accessible to sampling). Not suitable for demersal or adhesive eggs.

  2. Defined spawning area: Spawning concentrated in identifiable, accessible location. Not dispersed throughout stock range.

  3. Synchronous spawning: Most females spawn within defined period (season/month).

  4. Steady-state stock: Population not experiencing catastrophic recruitment failure or exceptional year-classes.

Data Assumptions:

  1. Accurate fecundity (\(F\)): Must be determined from gonad analysis of spawning females. Not from historical literature.

  2. Accurate sex ratio (\(R\)): Sampled directly from spawning grounds, not from general population.

  3. Accurate spawning fraction (\(S\)): Requires histological analysis or mark-recapture on spawning ground.

  4. Representative \(P_0\) sampling: Survey must cover entire spawning area, account for spatial patchiness.

Oceanographic Assumptions:

  1. Negligible egg mortality: Assumes eggs survive until next sampling occasion (typically <24 hours).

  2. No egg advection: Spawned eggs remain in survey area (weak currents).

If assumptions violated:

  • SSB estimates become unreliable
  • Catch recommendations may be inappropriate
  • Use sensitivity analysis or combine with other methods (acoustic, VPA, CPUE)

Question 6: Comparative Assessment

Compare DEPM with other spawning stock biomass estimation methods (mark-recapture, acoustic surveys, VPA). What are relative advantages and disadvantages?

Answer:

Method Data Needs Cost Accuracy Time Scale Best For
DEPM Fecundity, sex ratio, egg production High High 1-2 seasons Small pelagics with defined spawning
Acoustic Calibrated echo sounder High Medium Days-weeks Pelagic schools, real-time
Mark-Recapture Tagging program Medium Medium Weeks-months Discrete populations, movement
VPA Catch-at-age, M estimate Low Medium Long-term Multiple data sources, retrospective

DEPM Advantages: - Direct spawning stock estimate (not index) - Theoretically sound biological basis - Works when catch data unreliable

DEPM Disadvantages: - Very data-intensive - Sensitive to parameter uncertainty - Species-specific requirements - Expensive and labor-intensive

Recommendation: Use DEPM combined with 1-2 other methods for cross-validation.

Question 7: Real Data Problem

Given the following data from a Zanzibar anchovy spawning survey:

  • Survey area: 2,500 km²
  • Average daily egg production: 320 eggs/m²
  • Sex ratio: 0.50
  • Spawning fraction: 0.30
  • Batch fecundity: 8,500 eggs/female
  • Survey duration: 5 days
  • Total catch during survey: 150 tonnes

Calculate:

  1. Spawning stock biomass
  2. Exploitation rate (Catch/SSB)
  3. Is this sustainable? (Assume target exploitation rate = 10%)

Answer:

a) Calculate SSB:

\[SSB = \frac{320 \times 2,500 \times 10^6}{0.50 \times 0.30 \times 8,500}\]

\[SSB = \frac{8 \times 10^{11}}{1,275} = 6.27 \times 10^8 \text{ eggs}\]

Converting to biomass (anchovy eggs ~0.75 mg per egg): \[6.27 \times 10^8 \text{ eggs} \times 0.00075 \text{ g/egg} = 4.70 \times 10^8 \text{ g} = 470 \text{ tonnes}\]

SSB = 470 tonnes

b) Exploitation Rate:

\[E = \frac{\text{Catch}}{\text{SSB}} = \frac{150}{470} = 0.319 = 31.9\%\]

c) Sustainability Assessment:

  • Current exploitation: 31.9%
  • Target exploitation: 10%
  • Conclusion: Stock is overexploited at current fishing pressure. Catch should be reduced to ~47 tonnes to meet the 10% target exploitation rate
  • This indicates unsustainable harvest levels and need for management intervention
