Fish Population Dynamics and Stock Assessment

SECTION A: Unit Stock (Lecture 5)

Question 1.1 (Conceptual)

Define a unit stock and explain the key characteristics that distinguish one unit stock from another.

Model Answer:

A unit stock is a population of fish (single or multiple species) grouped together for assessment and management purposes that shares a common gene pool and occupies a defined geographical area.

Key characteristics: - Common gene pool – Can interbreed and maintain genetic continuity - Defined geographic area – Specific distribution boundaries and habitat - Similar life history traits – Comparable growth, reproduction, and mortality rates - Common fishing pressures – Subject to same fisheries and exploitation patterns - Managed as single entity – Can be independently managed and assessed

Significance: Unit stocks allow management of complex multispecies tropical fisheries by dividing populations into manageable units for assessment and conservation.

Question 1.2 (Application)

You are tasked with determining whether yellowfin tuna in the Indian Ocean constitute one stock or multiple unit stocks. What methods would you use, and what data would you collect?

Model Answer:

Methods for stock determination:

  1. Genetic analysis – DNA markers, microsatellites to assess gene flow and population structure
  2. Morphometric analysis – Vertebrae counts, scale patterns, size-at-age differences
  3. Parasitology – Parasite species/intensity indicating isolation
  4. Otolith microchemistry – Chemical signatures reflecting spawning area origin
  5. Tagging studies – Track movements and migration patterns

Data to collect: - Tissue samples from throughout Indian Ocean - Spawning location records (time, area, temperature) - Growth rates at different locations - Catch composition and fishing effort data - Environmental data (temperature, currents, productivity)

Expected outcomes: Multiple stocks likely exist (e.g., eastern, western Indian Ocean; seasonal variations)

Question 1.3 (Calculation)

A lake contains an estimated 50,000 tilapia. A survey found spawning occurring in two separate locations with different substrate types. Genetic analysis shows 85% within-population similarity but only 42% between-population similarity. Should these be managed as one or two unit stocks?

Model Answer:

Analysis: - Within-population similarity (85%) indicates relatively homogeneous genetics - Between-population similarity (42%) suggests significant differentiation and limited gene flow - Separate spawning locations with different habitats indicate ecological separation

Recommendation: Manage as TWO UNIT STOCKS

Rationale: - Low between-population genetic similarity (<50%) indicates reproductive isolation - Separate spawning habitats with different substrates suggest adapted populations - Independent management prevents mixing strategies and allows location-specific interventions - May have different growth rates, maturity ages, vulnerability to fishing

Question 1.4 (Essay)

Discuss the importance of properly identifying and delineating unit stocks for fisheries management in Tanzania’s coastal waters. Include examples of straddling and highly migratory species.

Model Answer:

Importance of unit stock identification:

  1. Accurate Assessment – Proper stock delineation ensures abundance estimates reflect actual population boundaries, not artificial divisions

  2. Sustainable Harvesting – Prevents overfishing by matching catch limits to correct population size. Mixing stocks artificially inflates perceived population

  3. Management Independence – Allows location-specific regulations (closed seasons, gear restrictions) suited to each stock’s dynamics

  4. International Coordination – Critical for stocks crossing borders (EEZ boundaries)

Tanzanian Examples:

Straddling Species: - Yellowfin tuna – Exists in both Tanzania’s EEZ and high seas; managed by IOTC - Swordfish – Distributed across multiple EEZs; requires transboundary agreements - Kingfish – Moves between Tanzanian and Kenyan waters seasonally

Highly Migratory Species: - Bigeye tuna – Oceanic distribution throughout Indian Ocean - Albacore – Annual migrations across thousands of kilometers

Management Challenges: - Must coordinate with IOTC and neighboring countries - Catch limits set at regional level may not reflect Tanzania’s specific fishing pressure - Stock mixing complicates assessment of Tanzania’s impact on global stocks

Recommendation: Establish Tanzania-specific monitoring for shared stocks; coordinate assessment data with regional management bodies

SECTION B: Methods of Stock Abundance Estimation (Lectures 6-7)

Question 2.1 (Conceptual)

Distinguish between direct and indirect methods of estimating fish stock abundance. Provide examples of each and discuss their advantages and disadvantages.

Model Answer:

Direct Methods – Independent abundance estimates not dependent on catch data

Examples: Trawl surveys, acoustic surveys, mark-recapture, visual census

Advantages: - Independent of commercial fishery data (unaffected by reporting biases) - Can provide absolute population estimates - Give information on spatial distribution

Disadvantages: - High cost and equipment requirements - Limited temporal coverage - Species/habitat specific (not universally applicable) - Logistically demanding

Indirect Methods – Use commercial fishery data as index of abundance

Examples: CPUE, depletion methods, VPA

Advantages: - Cost-effective (uses existing data) - Long time series often available - Large spatial/temporal coverage - Real-time updates possible

Disadvantages: - Affected by fishing behavior changes - Technology changes over time bias trends - Confounded by effort standardization issues - May not reflect true population dynamics

Best Practice: Use combination of methods for cross-validation and uncertainty quantification

Question 2.3 (Application)

A fisheries manager in Zanzibar must choose an abundance estimation method for assessing small pelagic fish (sardines and anchovies). The fishery has: - 20 years of commercial catch data - Limited research budget - Recent acoustic survey equipment available - No historical age-structure data

Which method(s) would you recommend and why?

Model Answer:

Recommended approach: COMBINED METHOD

Primary: Acoustic surveys + CPUE standardization

Rationale:

  1. Acoustic surveys (PRIMARY)
    • Small pelagics form dense schools (ideal for acoustics)
    • Non-lethal monitoring allows repeated surveys
    • Provides spatial distribution information
    • Can estimate size distribution from target strength
  2. CPUE standardization (SECONDARY)
    • 20-year historical data valuable for trend analysis
    • Can use GLM/GAM to standardize for effort, vessel, season effects
    • Cost-effective complement to surveys
    • Provides long-term context
  3. Why NOT VPA:
    • Requires accurate age-structure data (not available)
    • Sensitive to terminal F assumptions
    • Better for demersal/single-species stocks

Implementation Plan: - Year 1-2: Conduct seasonal acoustic surveys (3-4 per year) to establish baseline - Standardize historical CPUE using regression models - Cross-validate acoustic estimates with CPUE indices - Establish confidence intervals around abundance estimates - Use combined estimates for catch recommendations

Question 2.5 (CPUE Standardization)

A longline fishery for yellowfin tuna in Tanzanian waters has 10 years of catch data. Raw CPUE (catch/hooks) shows declining trend. However, you notice: - Vessel technology improved (GPS, sonar, better refrigeration) - Fishing season shifted from Jan-June to Mar-August - Number of vessels increased from 5 to 25

How would you standardize CPUE to account for these factors? What statistical method would you use?

Model Answer:

Standardization Approach: Generalized Linear Model (GLM)

Model specification: \[\ln(\text{CPUE}) = \alpha + \beta_1 \text{Year} + \beta_2 \text{Season} + \beta_3 \text{Vessel\_Age} + \beta_4 \text{Technology} + \varepsilon\]

Factors to include: 1. Year – Captures stock abundance trend (primary variable of interest) 2. Season – Accounts for seasonal availability/migration (categorical: Jan-Jun vs Mar-Aug) 3. Vessel size/class – Standardize effort across different vessel types 4. Technology level – Include GPS, sonar availability as binary factors 5. Fishing ground – If spatial data available, include location

Alternative: Generalized Additive Model (GAM) - More flexible for non-linear relationships - Better captures smoothed trends over time - Allows interaction between factors

Interpretation: - β₁ (Year coefficient) = annual change in standardized CPUE - Negative β₁ = declining stock abundance (accounting for confounding factors) - Compare to raw CPUE to assess technology/behavior bias

Expected result: Standardized CPUE may show less decline (or even slight recovery) compared to raw CPUE, indicating technology improvements masked actual stock status

SECTION C: Fish Migration & Acoustic Methods

Question 3.1 (Terminology)

Define and provide examples for each migration category:

  1. Diadromous fish – subcategories: anadromous, catadromous, amphidromous
  2. Potamodromous fish
  3. Oceanodromous fish

Model Answer:

a) Diadromous – Migrate between marine and freshwater environments

  • Anadromous (born in freshwater, spawn in freshwater)
    • Examples: Salmon, sea trout, shad
    • Spawn in rivers, juveniles migrate to ocean, adults return to spawn
    • High investment in single spawning event
  • Catadromous (born in marine, spawn in marine)
    • Examples: Freshwater eel, milkfish
    • Spend most life in freshwater, migrate to sea to spawn
  • Amphidromous (no spawning migration)
    • Examples: Mullet, tilapia (some), ten-pounder
    • Larvae spawned in marine environment drift to freshwater
    • Settle and grow in freshwater as juveniles
    • No return migration to spawn

b) Potamodromous – Migrate wholly within freshwater

  • Examples: Trout, bream, coregonids (whitefish), Nile tilapia
  • Landlocked species with seasonal migrations
  • Migrate for spawning, feeding, or seasonal redistribution
  • Often triggered by water level/temperature changes

c) Oceanodromous – Migrate wholly within ocean

  • Examples: Cod, herring, capelin, yellowfin tuna, mackerel
  • Long-distance oceanic migrations (thousands of km)
  • Spawning grounds separate from feeding grounds
  • Highly migratory species (tuna, billfish) may cross ocean basins

Stock Assessment Implications: - Diadromous species require collaborative management between freshwater/marine authorities - Potamodromous species vulnerable to river barriers and freshwater habitat degradation - Oceanodromous species require international coordination (regional fisheries bodies like IOTC)

Question 3.2 (Acoustic Surveys - Theory)

Explain the principle of acoustic fish detection. Include: a) How echo sounders detect fish b) Why frequency selection matters c) The concept of target strength

Model Answer:

a) Echo sounder principle:

  1. Signal transmission – Transducer emits brief sound pulse into water
  2. Reflection – Sound reflects off objects different in density from water (fish swim bladders)
  3. Echo return – Reflected sound returns to receiver
  4. Distance calculation – Time delay × sound speed = distance to object
  5. Amplitude analysis – Echo strength indicates fish size/density

b) Frequency selection:

  • High frequency (>200 kHz) – Short wavelength, can detect plankton and small fish
  • Lower frequency (<200 kHz) – Penetrates deeper, detects larger fish, better range
  • Trade-off – Higher frequency = better resolution but shorter range
  • Species considerations – Small pelagics use 38-200 kHz; demersal surveys use 10-18 kHz

Example: - Sardine/anchovy surveys: 38-120 kHz (schools near surface, need resolution) - Demersal surveys: 10-18 kHz (greater depth penetration required)

c) Target Strength (TS):

\[TS = 20 \log_{10}(L) + b_{20}\]

  • TS = Acoustic reflectivity (dB)
  • L = Fish length (cm)
  • \(b_{20}\) = Species-specific constant (typically −70 to −72 dB)

Interpretation: - Larger fish have stronger target strength - Allows size discrimination from acoustic returns - Critical for converting acoustic backscatter to abundance estimates

Example values: - Herring (20 cm): TS ≈ -40 dB - Sardine (12 cm): TS ≈ -50 dB - Anchovy (10 cm): TS ≈ -55 dB

Question 3.3 (Acoustic Survey Design)

Design an acoustic survey for assessing small pelagic fish stocks off the coast of Dar es Salaam. Include: a) Survey design and spatial coverage b) Equipment specifications c) Data collection protocol d) Validation methods

Model Answer:

a) Survey Design:

Study Area: - Depth range: 0-200 m (continental shelf) - Area: 50 km × 100 km = 5,000 km² - Transect design: Parallel lines perpendicular to coast, 5 km spacing

Transects: - 20 transects, each 100 km long - Vessel speed: 10 knots (constant) - 3 survey lines at different times (replicate) - Seasonal design: 4 surveys per year (quarterly)

b) Equipment:

  • Echo sounder: Split-beam, calibrated quarterly
  • Frequency: 38 kHz or 120 kHz (for small pelagics)
  • Transducer: Mounted 1-2 m below hull
  • Data logging: Simultaneous GPS, depth, temperature, echo integration

c) Data Collection Protocol:

  1. Pre-survey: Calibrate echo sounder using copper sphere
  2. Transect: Log acoustic data continuously; record every 100 m
  3. Sampling: Collect biological samples (length, age, species ID) at strategic locations using trawls or nets
  4. Environmental: Record SST, salinity, turbidity, oxygen
  5. Post-survey: Verify data quality, conduct backup calibration

d) Validation:

  1. Biological sampling – Conduct midwater trawls to confirm acoustic detections match captured species
  2. Visual observation – Verify surface schools observed match acoustic targets
  3. Cross-validation – Compare acoustic estimates with:
    • Commercial CPUE data from same period
    • Previous year acoustic surveys
    • Larval/egg surveys (if available)
  4. Uncertainty estimation – Bootstrap or Bayesian methods to estimate confidence intervals around abundance

Expected Output: - Biomass estimate (tonnes) with 95% CI - Spatial distribution map - Size-frequency distribution - Recruitment indices (juvenile abundance)

SECTION D: Length-Weight Relationships (LWR)

Question 4.1 (Conceptual)

Define the length-weight relationship (LWR). Explain: a) The biological meaning of the equation \(W = aL^b\) b) What isometric growth means c) Why LWR is important in fisheries management

Model Answer:

a) LWR equation: \(W = aL^b\)

  • W = Weight (g or kg)
  • L = Length (cm or mm)
  • a = Condition coefficient (species and environment specific)
  • b = Growth exponent (allometric coefficient)

Biological interpretation: - Describes how weight scales with length across population - \(b = 3\) predicts isometric growth (weight increases with L³) - \(b ≠ 3\) indicates allometric growth (disproportionate weight-to-length ratio) - Typically fits power-law relationship due to 3D body structure

b) Isometric growth (\(b = 3\)):

  • Fish maintains constant body shape and density as it grows
  • Weight ∝ Length³ (volume relationship)
  • Example: Red snapper with b = 3.02

Allometric growth (\(b ≠ 3\)): - b > 3: Fish becomes proportionally heavier with growth (“deepening body”) - b < 3: Fish becomes proportionally lighter with growth (“thinning body”) - May reflect seasonal changes, sexual maturity, environmental conditions

c) Importance in fisheries management:

  1. Condition monitoring – Deviation from species-specific LWR indicates health/nutrition
  2. Stock assessment – Convert length data to biomass estimates
  3. Population dynamics – Combine with age-length keys for cohort analysis
  4. Size regulations – Establish minimum/maximum size limits based on weight-length trade-offs
  5. Market standards – Determine fish value/quality
  6. Climate impacts – Changes in slope/intercept may reflect environmental stress

Question 4.2 (Calculation)

Red snapper length-weight data collected from Tanzanian coastal waters show: - Sample size: 80 fish - Length range: 10-55 cm - Power-law regression: \(\ln(W) = -2.45 + 3.02 \times \ln(L)\) (R² = 0.94)

Calculate: a) Parameter a and b b) Expected weight at L = 30 cm and L = 50 cm c) Condition factor K at each length d) Assess growth pattern (isometric or allometric)

Model Answer:

a) Extract parameters from regression:

From \(\ln(W) = -2.45 + 3.02 \times \ln(L)\):

  • \(\ln(a) = -2.45\), therefore a = e^{-2.45} = 0.086
  • b = 3.02

Equation: \(W = 0.086 \times L^{3.02}\)

b) Predicted weights:

At L = 30 cm: \[W = 0.086 \times 30^{3.02} = 0.086 \times 24,411 = 2,099 \text{ g} = 2.1 \text{ kg}\]

At L = 50 cm: \[W = 0.086 \times 50^{3.02} = 0.086 \times 125,893 = 10,827 \text{ g} = 10.8 \text{ kg}\]

c) Condition factor K:

Standard form: \(K = 100,000 \times \frac{W}{L^3}\) (or \(K = 10 \times \frac{W}{L^3}\) in relative form)

For L = 30 cm, W = 2.1 kg: \[K = \frac{1.0 \times 2100}{30^3} = \frac{2100}{27,000} = 0.078\]

For L = 50 cm, W = 10.8 kg: \[K = \frac{1.0 \times 10,800}{50^3} = \frac{10,800}{125,000} = 0.086\]

d) Growth pattern assessment:

  • b = 3.02 ≈ 3 → ISOMETRIC GROWTH
  • Fish maintains constant shape and density across size range
  • Weight increases proportionally to volume (L³)
  • Growth is homogeneous (no allometric deviation)
  • R² = 0.94 indicates excellent fit (94% of variation explained)

Biological interpretation: Red snapper shows uniform growth pattern; body proportions remain consistent from juvenile to adult

Question 4.3 (Application - Fisheries Management)

Compare two fish populations based on their LWR:

Population A (Lake Victoria Tilapia): - \(W = 0.018 \times L^{2.85}\)

Population B (Lake Tanganyika Cichlid): - \(W = 0.025 \times L^{3.15}\)

For fish of 25 cm length, calculate: a) Expected weights b) Condition factors c) Which population has better condition? Explain biological implications.

Model Answer:

a) Expected weights at L = 25 cm:

Population A: \[W_A = 0.018 \times 25^{2.85} = 0.018 \times 3,570 = 64.3 \text{ g}\]

Population B: \[W_B = 0.025 \times 25^{3.15} = 0.025 \times 9,441 = 236 \text{ g}\]

b) Condition factors:

Population A: \(K_A = \frac{64.3}{25^3} = 0.041\) (below standard isometric of 0.05)

Population B: \(K_B = \frac{236}{25^3} = 0.151\) (above standard)

c) Comparison and interpretation:

Metric Population A Population B Interpretation
b value 2.85 3.15 B has allometric growth (heavier)
Condition K 0.041 0.151 B in better nutritional condition
Growth pattern Negative allometry Positive allometry A losing weight proportionally; B gaining

Biological Implications:

Population A (Lake Victoria): - Negative allometry (b < 3) indicates fish becoming “thinned” with growth - Possible causes: - Nutritional stress or food scarcity - High fishing pressure on larger individuals - Environmental degradation - Management concern: Declining condition may reduce fecundity and survival

Population B (Lake Tanganyika): - Positive allometry (b > 3) indicates fish “deepening” with growth - Likely healthier: Better nutrition, adequate food resources - Positive sign: May reflect refuge status or lower fishing pressure - Population appears more robust for sustainable harvest

Management Recommendations: - Pop. A: Investigate food availability; consider harvest restrictions or habitat restoration - Pop. B: Can likely sustain higher fishing pressure; monitor for changes in condition

Question 4.4 (Statistical Analysis)

You collected LWR data on 100 sardines. Propose: a) Statistical methods to fit LWR b) How to test for isometry (b = 3) c) Uncertainty quantification approach

Model Answer:

a) Statistical fitting methods:

Method 1: Log-linear regression (Most Common) - Transform: \(\ln(W) = \ln(a) + b \times \ln(L)\) - Fit OLS regression on log-transformed data - Advantages: Linear model, easy interpretation - Disadvantages: Assumes log-normal error structure

Method 2: Power-law regression (Direct) - Fit \(W = aL^b\) directly using non-linear regression - Advantages: Models original scale; avoids log-transformation bias - Disadvantages: Requires iterative algorithms (R: nls(), nlme())

Method 3: Bayesian approach - Specify prior distributions for a and b - Update with likelihood from data - Advantages: Incorporate prior knowledge; full posterior uncertainty - Disadvantages: More computationally intensive

b) Test for isometry (H₀: b = 3):

Approach 1: Confidence interval - Fit regression, extract 95% CI for b coefficient - If CI contains 3 → isometric (fail to reject H₀) - If CI excludes 3 → allometric (reject H₀)

Approach 2: t-test \[t = \frac{b - 3}{SE_b}\] where \(SE_b\) = standard error of b coefficient

  • If |t| > critical value → significantly different from 3
  • Disadvantage: Low statistical power with this design

Approach 3: Constraint regression - Fit both models: (1) unconstrained; (2) constrained to b = 3 - Use likelihood ratio test to compare models - More powerful than simple t-test

c) Uncertainty quantification:

Method 1: Confidence intervals - Extract 95% CI for parameters a, b from regression output - Propagate to predicted weight: 95% CI on weight at each length

Method 2: Prediction intervals - Wider than confidence intervals (include prediction error) - Represents range for individual fish predictions

Method 3: Bootstrap - Resample 100 sardines (with replacement) 1000 times - Fit LWR to each resample - Calculate percentile CI for a and b from bootstrap distribution

Method 4: Bayesian posterior samples - Generate posterior samples of (a, b) - Compute credible intervals (typically 95%) - More intuitive interpretation: P(b = 3 | data)

Recommended approach: Log-linear regression with bootstrap CI on parameters

SECTION E: Integrated Assessment

Question 5.1 (Synthesis - All Topics)

You are assigned to conduct a stock assessment for yellowfin tuna in Tanzanian EEZ. The stock is: - Highly migratory (oceanodromous) - Managed by IOTC (Indian Ocean Tuna Commission) - Recently showing declining CPUE trends - Subject to both artisanal and industrial fishing

Develop a comprehensive assessment plan including: a) Unit stock definition and boundaries b) Methods for abundance estimation (justify selection) c) Data requirements d) Management recommendations

Model Answer:

a) Unit stock definition:

Indian Ocean Yellowfin Tuna (Thunnus albacares):

  • Geographic scope: Entire Indian Ocean (Tanzania’s EEZ is part of larger stock)
  • Distribution: Pelagic, oceanodromous; seasonal distribution patterns
  • Genetic unity: Single panmictic population throughout Indian Ocean
  • Management boundary: IOTC statistical areas (5°S-20°E to 25°S-90°E)
  • Spawning areas: Multiple locations; seasonal convergence off Somalia, Mozambique

Implication for Tanzania: Cannot manage independently; requires IOTC coordination

b) Abundance estimation methods (Combined approach):

Primary Methods: 1. Acoustic surveys (2 per year, 2-week duration each) - Target surface schools off Dar es Salaam, Zanzibar - 38 kHz frequency (pelagic small-fish optimized) - Covers shelf and slope regions

  1. CPUE standardization (commercial longline data)
    • 20 years available data from industrial fleet
    • Standardize for vessel type, technology, season, area
    • Use GLM with year effect as abundance index
  2. Mark-recapture (tagging program, ongoing)
    • Tag ~2,000 juvenile yellowfin annually
    • Provides movement, survival, migration data
    • Complements acoustic/CPUE estimates

Secondary Methods: 4. Egg/larval surveys (seasonal, if budget allows) - DEPM during peak spawning (Sept-Nov) - Estimates spawning stock biomass independent of catch

c) Data requirements:

Data Type Source Frequency Priority
Acoustic backscatter Research vessel surveys Quarterly High
Commercial catch Landing records Daily High
Fishing effort Logbooks (GPS tracks) Daily High
Biological (length, age) Observer program Continuous High
Environmental (SST, currents) NOAA/Sentinel Daily Medium
Tagging data Research program Annual Medium
Spawning indicators Gonad analysis Quarterly Medium

d) Management recommendations:

Short-term (0-2 years): - Current status assessment: Combine all methods for baseline estimate - Catch limit: Set at 80-90% of maximum sustainable yield (MSY) pending full assessment - Data collection: Intensify observer coverage (target 20% of boats monitored) - Regional coordination: Submit Tanzania data to IOTC working group

Medium-term (2-5 years): - Stock modeling: Develop integrated population model combining all data sources - Quota allocation: If stock shared, negotiate Tanzania’s share with IOTC - Sustainability: Implement seasonal closures during peak spawning if assessment indicates stress - Economic efficiency: License fewer high-tech vessels if CPUE standardization shows technology bias

Long-term (5+ years): - Ecosystem monitoring: Track small pelagics (food base), larger predators (tuna competitors) - Climate adaptation: Monitor oceanographic changes (upwelling intensity, distribution shifts) - Alternative livelihoods: Develop contingency plans if stock declines necessitate fleet reduction

Question 5.2 (Research Design)

Design a research project to investigate the relationship between ocean productivity (Chl-a from MODIS satellite data) and fish catch in Zanzibar coastal waters. Include:

  1. Hypothesis and specific objectives
  2. Sampling design (spatial, temporal)
  3. Data collection protocol
  4. Statistical analysis approach

Model Answer:

a) Hypothesis and objectives:

General Hypothesis: Ocean productivity (chlorophyll-a concentration) is positively related to fish catch in Zanzibar coastal waters, with variable lag between productivity changes and catch response.

Specific Objectives: 1. Quantify spatial-temporal patterns of Chl-a in Zanzibar EEZ 2. Characterize monthly variation in fish catch across coastal zones 3. Determine correlation between Chl-a and catch (synchronous and lagged) 4. Identify seasonal drivers of productivity-catch relationship

b) Sampling design:

Spatial: - Divide Zanzibar coastal zone into 5 grid cells (5°S-7°S; 39°E-42°E) - 1 km² resolution for satellite data; aggregate to 10 km for analysis - Focus on shelf waters (0-200 m depth)

Temporal: - Time period: 2015-2025 (10 years complete data coverage) - Satellite data: Monthly MODIS Aqua Chl-a (4 km resolution) - Catch data: Monthly landing records from fish markets (3 major ports) - Frequency: Monthly aggregations for analysis; seasonal analysis secondary

c) Data collection protocol:

Satellite data (MODIS Aqua): - Download monthly L3 standard mapped images from NASA Earthdata - Extract Chl-a values (mg/m³) for 5 grid cells - Quality control: Remove cloud-covered pixels, interpolate if <20% missing - Calculate zonal means and standard deviation

Catch data: - Survey landing sites: Dar es Salaam (main port), Zanzibar City, Bagamoyo - Record: Date, fish species, weight landed, vessel type, fishing area - Aggregate by month and grid cell using GPS coordinates if available - Cross-check against Ministry fisheries records

Environmental covariates: - Sea surface temperature (SST) from MODIS - Photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) - Wind speed (from NOAA reanalysis)

d) Statistical analysis:

Step 1: Descriptive analysis - Time series plots of Chl-a and catch (overall and by grid cell) - Seasonal decomposition (trend + seasonal + residual components) - Calculate mean, SD, CV for each month/grid cell

Step 2: Correlation analysis

Synchronous (same month): \[\text{Catch}_t \sim \text{Chl-a}_t\]

Lagged (productivity leads catch): \[\text{Catch}_t \sim \text{Chl-a}_{t-1}, \text{Chl-a}_{t-2}, \text{Chl-a}_{t-3}\]

  • Calculate Pearson/Spearman correlations
  • Test for significance (α = 0.05)
  • Identify optimal lag period

Step 3: Regression modeling

Model structure (Poisson GLM or linear regression): \[\ln(\text{Catch}_t) = \alpha + \beta_1 \text{Chl-a}_{t-\tau} + \beta_2 \text{SST}_t + \beta_3 \text{Season}_t + \varepsilon_t\]

where τ = optimal lag identified in correlation analysis

Predictors: - Chl-a at optimal lag - SST (thermal preference) - Seasonal dummy variables (Jan-March, etc.) - Interannual trends

Model diagnostics: - Check residual normality, homoscedasticity - Test for temporal autocorrelation (Durbin-Watson) - Assess multicollinearity (VIF < 5) - Cross-validation: Split data 80/20, assess prediction error

Step 4: Spatial analysis

Repeat regression by grid cell to identify geographic variation in productivity-catch relationship

Expected outcomes: - Significant positive Chl-a-catch correlation (lag 0-2 months expected) - Stronger relationship during upwelling season - Spatial heterogeneity in lag periods

Deliverables: - Spatio-temporal maps of Chl-a and catch - Correlation/regression summary table - Predictive model for catch forecasting based on current Chl-a - Management recommendations for seasonal fishing timing
